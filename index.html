NYC Career & Technical Education Playbook

Empowering Schools to Deliver High-Quality CTE Programming

About CTE

Career and Technical Education (CTE) programs provide academic and technical instruction in the content areas of agriculture, business and marketing, family and consumer sciences, health sciences, trade and technical education, and technology education.

This instruction is delivered alongside Work-Based Learning experiences to high school students. NYC has one of the largest CTE portfolios in America, with nearly 300 CTE programs across almost 130 high schools and over 65,000 participating students.

Chancellor's Vision

Chancellor Kamar H. Samuels has articulated a clear vision for New York City Public Schools centered on three core priorities: safe, academically rigorous, and truly integrated schools. A Jamaican-born immigrant who began his career as a Bronx elementary school teacher, Chancellor Samuels brings more than two decades of experience — from classroom teacher to principal to superintendent in both Brooklyn and Manhattan — to the nation's largest school system.

Chancellor Samuels is committed to seeing every student as a whole person with enormous potential, not just a data point. His approach emphasizes listening to educators, respecting families, and building schools where students are engaged in rigorous work that prepares them for college, careers, and civic life. Career-connected learning — including CTE — sits squarely within this vision as a powerful engine for social mobility, giving students concrete pathways to economic opportunity regardless of background.

In his own words: "Equity is not an abstract idea. It's a set of choices we make together in policy. What matters is not just what we do — it's how we do it, by listening to educators, by respecting families, by seeing students, not just as data points, but as whole people with enormous potential."

Standard CTE Program Components

Every approved CTE program is built around five interconnected pillars:

Pillar 1

Program Approval

Formal NYSED approval and reapproval ensuring alignment with state standards and workforce expectations.

Pillar 2

Curriculum Development

Industry-aligned curriculum mapped to NYSED learning standards and current labor market demands.

Pillar 3

Work-Based Learning

Supervised real-world experiences (minimum 54 hours) deepening career awareness and preparation.

Pillar 4

Industry Partnerships

Active connections with employers, sector experts, and community organizations.

Pillar 5

Graduation & Endorsement

The 4+1 CTE Pathway and technical endorsement on the diploma, signaling college and career readiness to employers and postsecondary institutions.

Overview

Being a CTE Teacher

You have spent years — maybe decades — building expertise in your field. You know what it takes to succeed in your industry. Now you are bringing that into a classroom. That is not a small thing. It is one of the most valuable things a young person in New York City can encounter.

You are not just a teacher. You are a bridge.

CTE teachers occupy a position that no other educator in the building holds. You connect the academic world to the professional world — not as a concept, but as a lived reality. Your students are not learning about your field. They are beginning to enter it, with you as their guide.

That is a fundamentally different role from a traditional classroom teacher, and it calls for a fundamentally different way of thinking about what you do every day.

Two Identities, One Role

Most new CTE teachers describe a version of the same experience in their first year: they feel like an expert in their field but a beginner in the classroom. That discomfort is real — and it is also exactly right. You are a beginner in the classroom. You are also an expert that almost no one else in the building can replicate.

The goal is not to choose between those two identities. It is to hold both at once. The most effective CTE teachers never stop being industry professionals. They bring the standards, the language, the expectations, and the culture of their field into the room every single day. At the same time, they develop the craft of teaching — how to sequence instruction, how to reach students who are struggling, how to manage a lab or a kitchen or a studio — with the same seriousness they once brought to their professional work.

What you bring from industry

Real expertise

Current knowledge of what the field actually looks like. Professional networks. Industry standards. The ability to say "this is how it works in the real world" — and mean it.

What teaching adds

Pedagogical craft

How to make complex knowledge accessible. How to sequence skills. How to give feedback that builds confidence and competence. How to manage a room of 25 teenagers learning something hard.

ℹ
You will not have both fully in year one — and that is expected

The Transitional-A certification pathway exists precisely because NYSED recognizes that industry expertise and teaching craft are developed separately. Your first years are designed to be a learning period. Use your mentor, your program coach, and your colleagues. Nobody expects a first-year teacher — from any background — to have everything figured out.

What CTE Instruction Actually Looks Like

A CTE classroom is not a lecture hall. It is not a pure skills lab either. At its best, it is a place where industry-authentic work and rigorous academic content are woven together so tightly that students cannot tell where one ends and the other begins.

In practice, that means a few things that may feel unfamiliar coming from industry:

You teach to a standard, not just a task

In industry, you complete a project and move on. In your classroom, every project, every task, every demonstration is connected to a learning standard — a specific skill or body of knowledge a student is expected to master. Understanding your curriculum crosswalk (the document that maps your course to NYSED and industry standards) is one of the most important things you will do in your first year.

Mistakes are part of the instruction

In most industries, mistakes are costly. In your classroom, they are the raw material of learning. A student who burns the sauce, cuts the wire wrong, or writes a broken function is giving you — and themselves — valuable information. The question is not how to prevent all mistakes. It is how to use them deliberately.

You are building a professional culture, not just teaching skills

Workplace norms — punctuality, communication, accountability, professionalism — are not soft extras in a CTE classroom. They are part of the curriculum. For many of your students, your class is the first place they encounter genuine professional expectations. How you run your room teaches them as much as what you teach in it.

The relationship is different from industry mentorship

You may have mentored junior colleagues or interns before. Teaching adolescents is related to that — but it is not the same. Your students are still developing not just professionally but personally. The trust they extend you, and the trust you build with them, operates on a different level. It requires patience, consistency, and the willingness to meet students where they are, not where you wish they were.

Your Classroom and the World Beyond It

One of the most common disconnects in CTE programs is the gap between what happens in the classroom and what happens during Work-Based Learning. Too often, WBL is treated as something separate — an internship students do, hours that get logged, a requirement that gets fulfilled. That is a missed opportunity.

Work-Based Learning is not a supplement to your teaching. It is a continuation of it.

Think about what your students are doing at their WBL placements. They are encountering real versions of the skills, tools, and concepts you introduce in class. They are also encountering things you have not covered yet — industry practices, professional dynamics, real problems with real stakes. When you know what your students are doing in their placements, you can design instruction that prepares them for what they are about to experience, and then debrief what they actually encountered. That loop — classroom, workplace, classroom — is where the deepest learning happens.

💡
A simple shift that changes everything

Ask your students what they did at their placement this week before you start your lesson. Not as a check-in — as instruction. What they bring back from the workplace is content. Use it.

The Power of Connected Instruction

Your classroom does not exist in isolation from the rest of the school — even though it can feel that way. The skills and concepts you teach have deep roots in academic subjects your students are studying simultaneously. When those connections are made visible to students, something remarkable happens: content that felt abstract in one class suddenly makes sense, and content that felt overly technical in yours suddenly has a context that makes it matter.

Consider a culinary arts teacher introducing students to recipe scaling. The math is right there — ratios, proportions, unit conversion, fractions. But if the culinary teacher and the math teacher have never spoken, the student experiences these as two unrelated things: "culinary math" and "real math." When those teachers are in conversation — even briefly — they can time instruction so that students encounter the concept in both settings close together. The reinforcement is powerful. The student who could not grasp proportions in the abstract grasps them immediately when they are scaling a beurre blanc for forty covers.

This is not just a culinary example. It is the CTE model at its best:

Health Sciences

Biology + anatomy

The cell biology unit in a student's science class lands differently when they are also learning about tissue types in their nursing assistant program. Two teachers, one concept, twice the understanding.

Construction Technology

Geometry + building trades

A student who cannot visualize the Pythagorean theorem on paper will often understand it immediately when squaring a foundation or calculating a roof pitch.

Computer Science

Algebra + programming logic

Variables, functions, and conditional logic are algebra. When the ELA teacher is working on argument structure at the same time a CS teacher introduces conditional statements, students often make the connection themselves.

Business & Marketing

Economics + entrepreneurship

Supply and demand, cost-benefit analysis, and market research are both economics curriculum and the foundation of any business plan. The social studies teacher and the business teacher are often teaching the same concepts in different languages.

You do not need a formal program or a scheduled collaboration period to start making these connections. A five-minute conversation with a math teacher in the hallway — "I'm teaching measurement conversions next week, what are you doing in class right now?" — is enough to begin. The teachers who do this consistently find that their students perform better, that academic teachers start sending students to them for context, and that the walls between CTE and the rest of the school start to come down.

ℹ
You are not responsible for making all of this happen alone

Academic alignment, WBL integration, and cross-departmental collaboration are program-level goals — not individual teacher burdens. Your program coach and school leadership share responsibility for creating the conditions that make this possible. Your job is to stay curious about what your colleagues are teaching and to be open about what you are teaching. The rest grows from there.

CTE teachers are sometimes the most underestimated educators in a building. Their programs are housed in specialized labs. Their credentials look different from academic teachers'. Their students are sometimes seen as kids who couldn't cut it in the college-track classes — a perception that is both wrong and worth actively pushing back against.

The reality is the opposite. CTE programs are where some of the most rigorous, industry-authentic learning in the school happens. Your students are earning credentials, completing hours of work-based learning, building portfolios, and in many cases graduating with a technical endorsement on their diploma that signals real competency to employers and colleges alike. That is not a consolation prize. That is a remarkable achievement, and it is one you are central to.

Within the school, you will interact with several communities that are distinct to the CTE role:

Your professional community

Industry advisory board

A group of industry professionals who advise your program, connect students to opportunities, and serve as external reviewers during re-approval. They are also your link back to the field you came from. Cultivate these relationships — they are one of the things that keeps your program current and your instruction relevant.

Your student community

Career and Technical Student Organizations (CTSOs)

Organizations like HOSA, SkillsUSA, DECA, and others give students a professional identity beyond the classroom. They compete, lead, and connect with peers across the city and state. CTSOs are one of the most powerful engagement tools available to a CTE teacher — and one of the most underused.

Your support system

Borough Program Quality Manager / Program Pathway Coach

Your assigned Central Office contact. They support your program's compliance, connect you with resources, help you navigate the approval process, and serve as your direct line to the Office of Student Pathways. They are not inspectors — they are partners. Use them early and often.

Your growth framework

The CTE Quality Continuum

A framework that describes what high-quality CTE practice looks like across six dimensions. Not a checklist, not an evaluation rubric — a map. It shows you where your program is and what growth in each dimension looks like. Return to it regularly as you develop your practice.

What Success Looks Like — and When It Comes

In your first year, success is showing up, learning the systems, building relationships with your students, and surviving the parts that are harder than you expected. That is enough. The teachers who build the most powerful programs over time are rarely the ones who had it all figured out in September. They are the ones who stayed curious, asked for help without shame, kept one foot in their industry, and never stopped caring about whether their students were actually learning.

Your students will remember you not because you covered the curriculum. They will remember you because you showed them that someone who had actually done the thing they want to do believed they could do it too. That is the CTE teacher's irreplaceable contribution — and no amount of paperwork, deadlines, or crosswalk documentation changes that fundamental truth.

The sections that follow in this Playbook cover the compliance, process, and documentation dimensions of your role. They matter — but they are in service of the work described here, not the other way around.

Overview

Teacher Certification

CTE programs require qualified teachers with both academic knowledge and real-world industry experience. NYSED offers multiple pathways for industry professionals to transition into CTE teaching, with a focus on the Transitional-A certification pathway.

Understanding Transitional-A Certification

A Transitional-A (Trans-A) certificate is awarded by NYSED and enables qualified industry professionals to teach CTE courses while completing the requirements for an Initial Certificate. The Trans-A certification:

  • Allows teaching in grades 7–12
  • Is valid for three years
  • Requires progression to Initial Certification through completion of 9 credits of education coursework during the three-year period

Pathway Options for Trans-A Certification

Option A

Associate's degree or higher + 2 years industry experience

Option B

High school diploma + 4 years experience + CQST exam

Option C

Industry certification + 2 years experience

Option D

Teaching experience verified by principal

Option E

Industry-specific pathway (requirements vary by field — verify on the NYSED website)

Application Process

Choose your CTE industry area

Review your education and work experience. Identify the CTE field that aligns with your background and research current opportunities in NYC schools.

Select the appropriate pathway

Review the requirements for each option. Verify specific requirements for your chosen industry area on the NYSED website.

Prepare your documentation

Gather educational transcripts, industry credentials, and completed work verification forms from previous employers. Prepare for any required assessments (e.g., CQST exam for Option B).

Complete required workshops

All pathways require: Child Abuse Identification and Reporting, Dignity for All Students Act (DASA), and School Violence Prevention and Intervention.

Apply simultaneously to NYC DOE and NYSED

Submit the NYC DOE Teacher Application (Common Profile, CTE subject area, personal statement) and the NYSED TEACH System application concurrently.

Secure employment

A job offer is required to finalize the Trans-A certification. Once hired, your principal will work with HR to submit final documentation.

Core Requirements

Program Approval

All CTE programs in New York City Public Schools must be formally approved by NYSED to ensure alignment with state regulations, workforce expectations, and the requirements for the 4+1 CTE Pathway, technical endorsement, and integrated/specialized academic credit.

ℹ
Approval is required to award credit and endorsements

Without active NYSED approval, schools cannot offer integrated/specialized academic credits, award the technical endorsement, or report student CTE data through SIRS Level 2 reporting.

Required Components Checklist

Every program approval or reapproval application must include all of the following:

  • Self-study committee documentation and meeting minutes
  • External review feedback with industry partner signatures
  • Curriculum crosswalk to NYSED learning standards and industry competencies
  • Work-Based Learning plan (minimum 54 hours documented)
  • Employability profile (current, documenting student achievements)
  • Three-part technical assessment details (written, performance, project)
  • Signed articulation agreement(s) with postsecondary partner(s)
  • Teacher certification evidence for all instructional staff

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Meeting agendas submitted without detailed minutes — notes must reflect meaningful engagement and decision-making

NYC CTE Playbook — Office of Student Pathways Program Approval
Core Requirements

Technical Assessment

Technical assessments are a critical component of NYSED-approved CTE programs. A technical assessment is an industry-developed assessment consisting of three parts that measure proficiency in a specific technical field through the application of national standards.

⚠
All three components are required — no exceptions

General employability skills tests cannot be used as the written examination or performance components (except barbering programs). These measure general workforce readiness, not specific technical knowledge.

The Three Required Components

Component 1

Written Examination

A cognitive knowledge assessment using multiple-choice and/or constructed-response questions reflecting the comprehensive program of study. Must be industry-developed by a third-party vendor. Measures theoretical knowledge across all program curriculum.

Component 2

Performance Assessment

A performance examination where students demonstrate practical skills in the technical field. Must be industry-developed by a third-party vendor. Evaluates hands-on abilities and application of technical knowledge.

Component 3

Student Project / Local Assessment

A culminating project or portfolio of artifacts that demonstrates in-depth technical knowledge. Locally determined and developed by the school or program. Shows comprehensive understanding through sustained work. A rubric with a defined passing grade must be attached.

Acceptable Assessment Types

  • Industry Certification Exams — Tests administered by industry organizations (e.g., CompTIA, AWS, ASE, NOCTI)
  • NYS Licensing Examinations — Required where applicable by law (e.g., Appearance Enhancement, Barbering)
  • Third-Party Vendor Assessments — Professionally developed technical exams from educational assessment providers
  • Local Consortium Option — Only available when no industry assessment exists for the specific field; requires documentation of industry partner participation

Selection Criteria

When selecting a technical assessment, verify it meets all five criteria:

  • Alignment to Industry Standards — Developed by industry representatives aligned to current national standards
  • Comprehensive Coverage — Evaluates the entire program of study, not just one course
  • Current Relevance — Reflects current industry practices and technologies
  • Measurable Proficiency — Includes a clear cut score or rubric for determining proficiency
  • Appropriate Rigor — Challenges students while remaining accessible to well-prepared completers

Technical Endorsement Requirements

For students to receive a technical endorsement on their diploma, they must:

  • Complete all courses in an approved CTE program
  • Pass all three components of the technical assessment
  • Complete the required Work-Based Learning hours (minimum 54 hours)
  • Meet all other graduation requirements

Self-Study Reflection Questions

  • How was the industry-developed assessment chosen? What industry partners were involved?
  • If program changes have occurred, does the assessment still align with the program?
  • Does the local project rubric effectively measure student attainment of technical skills?
  • Are there adequate supports for students while maintaining assessment integrity?
  • How are assessment results used to improve program quality?

Work-Based Learning

Work-Based Learning (WBL) is a core component of Career and Technical Education that provides students with supervised, real-world learning experiences to deepen career awareness, exploration, and preparation. NYSED requires students in approved CTE programs to complete at least 54 hours of WBL experience.

Benefit 1

Professional Exposure

Exposure to professional environments and workplace norms before entering the workforce.

Benefit 2

Networking

Connecting students with mentors, employers, and industry professionals.

Benefit 3

Applied Learning

Application of classroom learning to authentic industry tasks and challenges.

Benefit 4

Skill Development

Development of leadership, communication, and professional soft skills.

The WBL Continuum

WBL opportunities exist along a continuum from career awareness through career preparation:

Career Awareness

  • Guest speakers
  • Workplace tours
  • Virtual career fairs

Career Exploration

  • Informational interviews
  • Job shadowing
  • Industry panels

Career Preparation

  • Paid/unpaid internships
  • Apprenticeships
  • CBO-based programs

Equity, Inclusion, and Accessibility

Equitable access to WBL means ensuring every student — regardless of background, identity, or ability — has meaningful opportunities. Key strategies include:

  • Remove barriers — Address challenges like unpaid internships and rigid schedules by offering multiple formats (remote, hybrid, in-person)
  • Support for ELLs & MLLs — Translate materials, offer interpreter services, and provide bilingual career guides
  • Universal Design for Learning — Offer choices in how students engage and demonstrate learning; use accessible digital tools
  • Cultural partnerships — Partner with cultural organizations and affinity-based mentors
  • Family engagement — Partner with parents via multilingual outreach and community information sessions

Developing a WBL Plan

Successful WBL requires strategic school-level implementation across five areas:

Planning & Scheduling

Align WBL with school goals and CTE pathways. Use school calendars to map out activities across the year.

Stakeholder Engagement

Collaborate with CBOs, industry partners, parents, and teachers. Build advisory councils and feedback loops.

Resource Allocation

Assign staff (WBL Coordinator, CTE teachers, Career Advisor) and dedicate time and tools for coordination.

Communication Strategy

Use newsletters, flyers, and town halls. Develop a student-facing WBL information resource.

Continuous Improvement

Use surveys, exit tickets, and employability profile data to refine efforts over time.

Self-Study Process

The Self-Study is the essential first step in the NYSED CTE program approval or re-approval process. In NYC Public Schools, this process provides a structured opportunity for internal school stakeholders to reflect deeply on the strengths and needs of their CTE program, ensuring it is aligned with both NYSED requirements and the realities of our city's dynamic workforce.

Purpose of the Self-Study

The self-study is a collaborative, school-driven process that allows CTE teams to:

  • Evaluate the program's alignment with New York State learning standards
  • Determine if the program continues to meet the career interests of NYC students
  • Ensure the program reflects current labor market trends
  • Assess whether the program is accessible, inclusive, and supported by school resources

Who Should Be Involved

The self-study committee should reflect diverse perspectives. Suggested membership includes:

Administration

School & District Leaders

Principals, assistant principals, CTE directors, and district administrators.

Instruction

CTE & Academic Teachers

CTE teachers from the program under review plus academic subject teachers if integrated credits are offered.

Guidance

Counselors & Advisors

Guidance counselors, college and career advisors.

Special Education

SpEd Coordinator

Special education coordinator or advocate to ensure equitable access.

Postsecondary

College Partners

Postsecondary partners from relevant colleges or universities.

Industry

Business & Workforce

Business, industry, or union partners plus a Workforce Development Board or Youth Council representative.

⚠
Important: Self-study members cannot serve on the External Review Committee

To preserve the integrity of both processes, individuals who participate in the self-study may not also serve on the external review committee.

What the Self-Study Evaluates

The committee should meet multiple times over several months to evaluate these components:

  • District Commitment — Administrative and counseling support; alignment with school-wide goals
  • Labor Market Relevance — Alignment with current or emerging industry demands in New York City
  • Accessibility & Equity — Access for SWD, ELLs, and MLLs; plans to address barriers
  • Feasibility — Staffing, facilities, and equipment sufficient for high-quality instruction
  • Student Enrollment & Impact — Sufficient student interest; scheduling accommodations
  • Curriculum Mapping — Course alignment to NYSED standards and industry competencies
  • Work-Based Learning — Meaningful WBL opportunities; valid affiliation agreements (health programs)
  • Employability Profile — Current profile documenting student achievements
  • Assessment Readiness — Valid three-part technical assessments embedded in the program
  • Articulation Agreements — Signed and current postsecondary credit agreements
  • Teacher Certification — Valid NYSED certification for all instructional staff

Timeline & Documentation

For NYC public high schools, the self-study process typically spans six months to one year, depending on program complexity. Schools must document the entire process — not just the outcomes — including:

  • Meeting minutes — Topics discussed, decisions made, and recommended program modifications
  • Self-study reports — Findings, conclusions, and next steps
  • Curriculum crosswalks — Supporting documentation for each required component
✕
Meeting agendas alone are not accepted as documentation

Notes must reflect meaningful engagement and decision-making. These materials must be submitted alongside the program's approval or re-approval application to NYSED and will be reviewed by the External Review Committee.

People & Process

External Review

As part of the NYSED CTE program approval and re-approval process, NYC public high schools are required to conduct an External Review. This process ensures that each CTE program aligns with current industry standards, meets NYSED criteria, and responds to the unique needs of NYC students, schools, and local labor markets.

Purpose of the External Review

Function 1

Review the Self-Study

Committee members assess the self-study to ensure the program is academically rigorous, relevant, and reflective of current industry practices.

Function 2

Identify Gaps

The committee highlights deficiencies — outdated content, missing standards, or unclear assessment strategies — that need to be addressed.

Function 3

Recommend Strategies

Members provide concrete suggestions from industry and education partners to strengthen the program and align it with NYC's economic landscape and evolving workforce needs.

Key Definitions

Self-Study Committee
A local panel formed by the school that includes NYC educators, business/industry partners, community members, and relevant experts.
Gaps
Areas where the program falls short of state requirements or NYC workforce expectations — missing content, outdated technologies, or misalignment with employer demands.
State Criteria
NYSED CTE learning standards and programmatic requirements that every approved program must meet.
Local Needs
The specific educational, workforce, and community needs of New York City and its school districts.

Application Process Walkthroughs

Walkthrough 1: Preparing for Re-Approval (12–18 months out)

Objective: Launch the process early with the right team and timelines.

  • Form a re-approval leadership team (CTE Teacher, WBL Coordinator, Administrator, Guidance Counselor)
  • Set up a shared Google Drive folder using the "CTE Re-Approval Toolkit" structure
  • Review the expiration date of current program approval in the NYSED database
  • Identify program updates since last approval (new courses, staff, WBL opportunities)
  • Draft an internal project timeline to meet the June 30 submission deadline

Walkthrough 2: Conducting the Self-Study (10–6 months out)

Objective: Reflect internally on the program's quality and compliance.

  • Create a self-study committee (CTE and academic teachers, admin, student reps, SWD/ELL advocates)
  • Review curriculum crosswalk to NYS standards, teacher certifications, articulation agreements, employability profiles, and equity and access for all students
  • Keep minutes of every meeting
  • Draft a self-study summary to share with your external review team
💡
NYCPS Tip

Be honest about program gaps — addressing them proactively strengthens your case for approval.

Walkthrough 3: Hosting the External Review (6 months out)

Objective: Receive feedback from industry experts and stakeholders.

  • Invite external reviewers: 2 industry representatives, postsecondary partner, academic teachers, community members
  • Share the self-study summary in advance
  • Facilitate breakout sessions by content area if reviewing multiple programs
  • Collect signed participation forms or emails with role and feedback
  • Document all suggestions and final recommendations
💡
NYCPS Tip

Present student work samples and WBL partners during the external review session — it demonstrates program quality in action.

Supporting Students with Disabilities

Students with disabilities represent a significant portion of the CTE student population. According to NYSED data, students with disabilities make up 21.7% of all CTE concentrators in New York State — higher than their representation in the overall secondary student population (18.4%). CTE programs can be particularly attractive and beneficial for students with disabilities.

How CTE Helps Students with Disabilities

Hands-on learning

Applied approaches align well with the learning needs of many students with disabilities, providing alternative pathways to demonstrate knowledge and skills.

Marketable skills

Development of skills that can lead to competitive employment opportunities, supporting economic independence.

Self-advocacy

Authentic work experiences through WBL help students develop self-advocacy skills and understand workplace accommodations before full employment.

Multiple pathways

Various assessment approaches accommodate different learning styles and needs, providing multiple routes to earning credentials.

Inclusion Strategies

  • Collaborate with Special Education Professionals — Understand each student's IEP or 504 plan and ensure accommodations are provided consistently
  • Design Accessible Learning Spaces — Consider physical layout, sensory stimuli, and placement of equipment and materials
  • Use Inclusive Language — Person-first language (e.g., "students with disabilities" rather than "disabled students")
  • Create a Culture of Acceptance — Address misconceptions, model inclusive behaviors, and foster peer relationships
  • Involve Students in Program Planning — Encourage self-advocacy and opportunities for students to share accommodation preferences
  • Establish Clear Safety Protocols — Address needs of students with various disabilities while maintaining high behavioral expectations

Teaching Strategies

  • Universal Design for Learning (UDL) — Multiple means of engagement, representation, and action/expression
  • Scaffold Complex Tasks — Break tasks into smaller steps; provide visual supports like checklists and diagrams
  • Assistive Technology — Use tools appropriate for both classroom and workplace contexts
  • Multisensory Approaches — Engage visual, auditory, and kinesthetic modalities

Supporting WBL for Students with Disabilities

ℹ
Legal framework: Compliance with IDEA and ADA is non-negotiable

WBL activities must support students' IEP goals. Work with special educators and counselors to align WBL experiences with transition services.

  • Flexible workplace arrangements — Flexible hours, job coaching, assistive technology at the placement site
  • Pre-placement assessment — Match student interests and abilities to appropriate placements
  • Vocational fit and reflection — Use tools like SACC portfolios and journaling to track preferences and performance

Curriculum & Instruction

High-quality CTE curriculum is built around four interconnected elements: industry standards, curriculum design, project-based learning, and student skill development. Courses must be mapped to both NYSED learning standards and current industry competencies, with regular review to ensure alignment with labor market changes.

Industry Partnerships

Strong industry partnerships are the foundation of a high-quality CTE program. They connect schools to businesses, community organizations, and government agencies — providing students with access to real-world expertise, work-based learning opportunities, and industry-valued credentials. Partners should be actively involved in curriculum advisory boards, external reviews, and WBL coordination.

Effective college and career advising moves through four stages:

Exploration

Discover interests and goals through career assessments, informational interviews, and career cluster exploration.

Planning

Develop academic and career plans aligned with student interests and postsecondary goals.

Application

Submit college and job applications with support from dedicated college and career advisors.

Transition

Prepare for career entry or further education with job readiness skills and postsecondary planning.

Glossary & Resources

Key Terms

Articulation Agreement
A formal agreement between a high school CTE program and a postsecondary institution that outlines college credit, advanced standing, or course equivalency for high school coursework.
Career and Technical Education (CTE)
An educational approach that integrates academic instruction with technical and career-specific training to prepare students for college, careers, and lifelong learning.
Career Cluster
A grouping of related career pathways in a particular industry or field (e.g., Health Sciences, Information Technology, Business Management).
CDOS
Career Development and Occupational Studies — NYS Learning Standards guiding career readiness instruction, with Option 1 and Option 2 pathways for meeting graduation requirements.
Commissioner's Regulations (CR 100.5)
The official NYSED regulation outlining graduation requirements, including those related to CTE program approval and technical endorsements.
CTE Program Approval
A formal NYSED process verifying that a CTE program meets all state-required components to offer integrated credit, technical assessments, and the CTE 4+1 graduation pathway.
CTE Technical Assessment
A three-part exam (written, performance, and industry-recognized) used to evaluate a student's readiness for a career in their CTE field.
Employability Profile
A cumulative document recording a student's technical skills, soft skills, licenses, and WBL hours. Used to support job placement, internships, and postsecondary applications.
SCED Code
School Courses for the Exchange of Data — a five-digit standardized course identifier used in NYSED SIRS reporting and program approval documentation.
SIRS
Student Information Repository System — the NYSED database where student CTE data is reported at Level 2.
Transitional-A Certificate
A three-year NYSED teaching certificate enabling qualified industry professionals to teach CTE while completing education coursework toward Initial Certification.
Work-Based Learning (WBL)
Supervised, real-world learning experiences that deepen career awareness, exploration, and preparation. NYSED requires a minimum of 54 hours for CTE concentrators.
